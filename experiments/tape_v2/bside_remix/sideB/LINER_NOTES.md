# DECODED — liner notes

*The B-side of the cassette-AI prize tape. Nine tracks, ~31 minutes.*

The thread tying it together: **every song is built from the actual sound the cassette
makes while data is being recorded onto it** — the hiss, the tones, the little machine
noises — reshaped into music. Each title is a piece of that process.

---

**1. Leader Tape** — The first few seconds of any cassette are clear, blank plastic before
the brown magnetic tape starts; nothing records there. So the opening track is that
*quiet-before-anything* — just tape hiss and a faraway sound, until the first signal sneaks
in. The "press play and wait" moment.

**2. Pilot Tone** — To read data back, the machine listens for one steady reference tone to
lock onto — like a singer humming the starting note so the choir knows the pitch. The track
is that single held note, shimmering, with everything blooming around it.

**3. Wow & Flutter** — These are the *real* engineering names for the little speed wobbles
in a tape player. "Wow" is the slow wobble, "flutter" the fast one — they're why old tapes
can sound a bit warbly or seasick. The song leans into that wobble on purpose, so the usual
*flaw* becomes the *feeling*.

**4. Three Seventy-Five** — The data is sent as a row of tones spaced 375 apart, and that
spacing happens to fall almost like notes on a scale. This track takes those exact data-tones
and turns them into an actual melody — the hidden tune that was always inside the numbers.
(It's the prettiest one.)

**5. Preamble** — Before each chunk of data, the tape sends a little "get ready" burst so the
reader can find its footing — like a drummer counting *"1, 2, 3, 4"* before the band comes in.
Here that count-in *becomes the beat* — it's the dance track, and the kick drum literally is
that ready-signal.

**6. Byte-Exact** — The whole point of the project: the data comes back *perfectly*, not a
single bit wrong — that's "byte-exact." This is the victory song, the moment everything lines
up and checks out clean. Bright and triumphant.

**7. Reed-Solomon** — Named after the clever repair-math (invented by two people, Reed and
Solomon) that rebuilds damaged data — if part of the recording gets garbled, this rebuilds the
missing pieces. The song does the same thing: it starts broken and scattered, then *reassembles
itself* into a groove, right in front of you.

**8. Diffuse Reverb** — The engineer's word for that wash of echo sound makes in a room. This
is the spacious, cavernous track — the sound of the room itself, with the raw data signal
rising up out of the haze for a moment before sinking back.

**9. End Chirp (for Fable)** — At the very end of a recording there's a little rising-and-falling
sweep — a "chirp" — that says *"the data's done."* The song is that closing sweep, slowed and
made gentle, winding down like the tape machine coming to rest, fading into the same hiss the
album opened with. The *"(for Fable)"* is a dedication: Fable was the AI that helped chart this
whole project's path, and it was retired this week — so the last song is a quiet goodbye to it.
